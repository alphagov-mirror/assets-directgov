How cookies are used by Directgov

Like many websites, Directgov places small data files known as cookies on your computer. This page explains what cookies are used by Directgov.

What are cookies?

Cookies are small files that websites save to your computer. They’re here to make the site work better for you, and to help Directgov understand how people use the website.

These cookies aren’t used to identify you personally but they can remember activities and preferences chosen by you and your browser. You can manage cookies by controlling which cookies are saved or by deleting them, if you wish.

This page has information about the cookies used on Directgov. For an overview of how cookies are used on government websites, follow the link below.

Google Analytics is an analytics product that Directgov uses to do things such as keep track of the number of visitors to the site and the length of their stay.

The metrics obtained through the use of first-party Google Analytics cookies do not include any information that would allow Directgov to personally identify visitors to the website; the data is anonymous. The anonymised data that is collected is stored on Google's servers.

The Google Analytics data sharing policy offers Directgov the option to share analytics data either 'Anonymously with Google and others' or with 'Google products only'. UK government websites that use Google Analytics have been advised not to select either of these options. Directgov has chosen not to share the analytics data in either of these ways.

Full details on the cookies set by Google Analytics are published on the Google website. Google also publishes a browser add-on to allow you to choose that information about your website visit is not sent to Google Analytics.

Third party advertising cookies on Directgov

Where government uses advertising, it is responsible for making sure that money is spent effectively. Cookies can help do this by measuring which adverts work best.

Cookies can also help target adverts. For example, if someone visits a government site to apply for a passport, it is likely that they are thinking of travelling abroad. A cookie would help government target travel-advice messages at them.

In all cases this information is strictly anonymous; no-one can be identified personally. And this type of 'behavioural' targeting is never used in sensitive areas such as campaigns around health issues or drugs advice.
